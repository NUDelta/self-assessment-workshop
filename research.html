Background Research

This workshop is grounded in our previous research. On this page, we present a brief summary and provide references for additional reading.

  • Previous research [2] has shown that students often self-assess their programming abilities negatively in moments that are typical in the programming process
    • For example, a student may believe they are doing poorly when they need to stop to plan on their assignment, even though their professors would expect them to do that as a part of the programming process.
    • Women also tend to be more self-critical of their programming abilities in these moments, and students of all genders tend to be more self-critical when comparing themselves to women [3]
  • Our recent work [1] has explored students' reasons for their self-assessments (e.g., they think that they should know enough to not need to plan), as well as *sources* of information for their self-assessment criteria (e.g., they see their peers work without planning)
    • We found that students cited their professors as powerful sources for self-assessment criteria, but these sources are not available for all self-assessment moments.
      • In lieu of information about what is expected of them as learners, students make assumptions from incomplete information about what is expected of them.
    • This motivates our desire to co-design with faculty interventions that leverage students’ trust in their professor to provide sources of self-assessment criteria to students in a scalable manner.
